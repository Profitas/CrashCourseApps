An Introduction to Relationship Dynamics

Before I can teach you the practical tools and tactics needed to get your boyfriend back, I need to outline some of the core reasoning behind it. This reasoning converges into a set of core principles that are irrefutably present in every male-female relationship that has ever occurred.

An understanding of these principles, in turn, allows you to better, and more intuitively understand precisely how the techniques I’m going to lay out in latter sections function. These principles are based on the underlying differences in psychology inherent to males and females, and are built upon the countless hours I’ve spent reading the latest psychological journals for cutting-edge, scientific, and highly practical research.

The first key principle to understand is that men and women are wired differently from one another. This one requires little explanation. While there are obviously some similarities between the two sexes, there is a gulf of differences in terms of how their brains function when it comes to understanding and forming relationships. Even the desired outcomes from a relationship differ greatly between males and females. As well, there are huge differences in the things that either sex finds attractive – and these function on a deep, psychological level.

In relationship science, the first difference that becomes clear is the role that men and women play in the courtship rituals. Traditionally, men have been the ones who initiate contact, begin making advances, and work hard to get the female’s attention. On the other hand, women have played the role of the selector in such relations. They get to choose which of the multiple males making advances they want, and can thus be more picky, more selective, in the men they chose to begin relationships with.

This principle has been hard-wired in the brains of both males and females by evolutionary science, and works completely to the advantage of the female. The woman had the freedom to choose the best looking, most intelligent, and most caring partner for her to begin a relationship with.

Our brains are deeply rooted to facilitating these roles. The only problem, for women, is that the huge advantage they traditionally gain at the start of a relationship begins to deteriorate as the courtship progresses. In many cases that I have seen, at a certain point in the relationship, the roles become completely reversed. The woman then finds herself pursuing the same man who was vying for her attention only weeks ago, and the grip of power goes completely back to the man.

Typically the trajectory is as follows. A man begins his pursuit of a woman, and the woman is both flattered and interested. She shows some initial restraint, but is soon won over by the man’s advances. The man, sensing this, takes advantage of the opportunity and begins making more aggressive moves on the advances. He takes her out, wines and dines her, and eventually goes to bed with her.

By this point, the woman feels comfortable in the man’s presence, and starts to want to win even more of her man’s approval. She begins to dress sexier, pretends to be interested in his hobbies, and frees up her schedule to spend more time with him. This is exactly the point where the tables turn.

The man is no longer pursuing;he has, in effect, won over the woman. Now, it is up to her to keep him interested, and she does so by trying to please him, either by the way she looks, or the things she does with him, or some other variation.  Think back, and try to remember a situation where you found yourself seeking your man’s approval.

This is the point where something about the female selector role changes. Originally, it was your ex-boyfriend who displayed interest in you, who pursued you, who chased you. Ultimately, you were the one to select him. Your desire for a relationship with him became overpowering, and you valued the promise of a long, healthy relationship more than the selection of the best possible man on your terms.

So, little by little, you began to try to foster a deeper relationship with him. You feigned interest in his activities, dressed yourself up to look good for him, catered to his wishes. However, at a certain point, such subservient behavior becomes unappealing to a man. This spirals out of control, and you lose the grip you had on your once-solid relationship. 

To regain control of a relationship, you have to clearly establish that you are the selector and he is pursuing you. You need to consistently work to reaffirm your feminine role of power in the relationship, because he is the one chasing you. You have to test him over and over to make sure he is a good fit for a long-term relationship.

The Most Important Thing In A Relationship, Is The Concept Of Value.

The Walking Power

A large proportion of women are afraid of their boyfriend’s disinterest. At our core, as fully functioning, emotional human beings, we all seek approval from the people we come in contact with. We are social, creative creatures with the desire to both dispel and take in love from our relationships. Perhaps you have once been, or are now, one of these women. For you, a concept that is of ultimate importance to understand is that of walking power.

Walking power is the power dynamic that you have to work to control in a relationship. The name is derived from your ability to “walk away”from the relationship. However, there are women who give their boyfriends the peace of mind of knowing that no matter how poorly they, the boyfriend, behave, the woman will never “walk away.”

Thus, there is a distinct lack of walking power in the relationship. If a man believes that no matter how poorly he acts, you will not walk away from the relationship, this knowledge gives him the tools necessary to push your boundaries and test your limits. As you’ve learned by now, women without boundaries have minuscule amounts of perceived value. The man you’re after will leave you, if you surrender completely to him.

You may become simply an ex-girlfriend, a casual girl, or a booty call, whatever you want to call it. But I know that that it is not why you bought this book. You want to avoid becoming that girl.

For this reason, have clear, definable boundaries in your head is of utmost importance. You need to be able to stand your ground, and have the willingness to walk away if those boundaries are crossed. Communicate this to your man. I know from boundless personal experience that he will respect you a lot more once he knows you are a woman who has strong boundaries.

Moreover, you absolutely cannot afford to show your boyfriend you are afraid of losing him. You must never be at a point, where losing him, becomes synonymous with the end of the world for you. That is not to say, of course, that you can’t be loving and affectionate, in fact, I encourage you to let him know that you care about him. 

There isn’t a single relationship that can last if your boyfriend thinks that he is meaningless to you.  However, he still needs to know that you will be fine and be able to move on if the relationship breaks. This grants you the larger portion of power.

The Downward Spiral of Behavior

The downward spiraling of behavior occurs when your boyfriend gets away with something that you would not otherwise let anybody else do. At the start of a relationship, we tend to put up with a lot less than later on, once trust and a rapport has been established.

The boundaries are less clear at the initial point, and so mistakes in behavior by your man can be rationalized, from his perspective, by not fully understanding the rules. This starting moment is a critical time in which to establish your boundaries. Have clearly defined rules for his behavior, and punish him appropriately when he steps over the line. Trust me, having this type of solidity early on, will do wonders in preventing misbehavior at later stages.

Additionally, avoiding downward spiraling of behavior, is absolutely crucial when defining your boundaries. Once your boyfriend commits a negative behavior, and is allowed to get away with it, it becomes much more difficult to get him out of that downward spiral. As they say, you can’t teach an old dog new tricks, and in this case, you can’t expect your man to change his behavior six months into a relationship, if he has been doing the same thing since the start. Otherwise, he’ll get used to you putting up with his antics, and take advantage of your demeanor. Over time, he’ll come to expect that type of passive compliance coming from you.

If you’re not aware of this process beforehand, it can get out of control very quickly. Most women tend to clue in at some point in the relationship, that their boyfriend is getting away with more than they normally tolerate, and think the best strategy is to confront them immediately about it. Unfortunately, this type of tactic is most of the time rendered useless. Your boyfriend will probably laugh it off, and continue with his negative behavior. Once the downward spiral of behavior has started, it’s nearly impossible to break out of it.

If you’ve experienced this in a relationship, you’ve likely even just encountered it in your current relationship, otherwise you wouldn’t be reading this book! You know how frustrating it can be. Once bad behavior starts, certain patterns develop in the relationship. Your boyfriend likely expected that you were cool with him behaving the way he does, and when you confront him, it seems, from his perspective, that it’s coming right out of the blue. This is when the first cracks in a relationship become visible, fighting starts, arguing becomes more common, and the comfort and trust begins to fade.

To avoid this downward spiral of behavior, you have to be firm in establishing ground rules from the outset of the relationship. If you let your boyfriend get away with behavior you don’t like in the opening stages of the relationship, it’s dishonest, again, from his perspective, to then decide to, almost arbitrarily, try to stop the behavior once the relationship has moved forward. He’ll feel cheated, and, much like a small child, rebel against your warnings, oftentimes only exaggerating the behavior that you confronted him about earlier. So, you have to establish your ground rules at the onset.

The same principles apply to your own behavior. Say you like going bowling with your girlfriends every Thursday night, or enjoy a Sunday night dancing at the clubs. Make sure to continue doing them in the beginning of a relationship. Your boyfriend will then see them as “normal”things that you do, and won’t ever protest as you keep doing them. However, if all of a sudden, six weeks into dating a guy, you start going out on the weekends to get drinks at the bards, he will immediately become suspicious, Is she cheating on me? He’ll think you’ve lost interest, and in turn be tempted to call the whole thing off, whereas in reality you were only continuing things you’ve enjoyed doing before meeting him! You need to set the standard early on, so that no problems are run into concerning your own behavior at later points. With this in place, you can continue to enjoy your own “girl time.”

You Gave Him Too Much For Too Little Effort

This is the most valuable advice I can bestow upon you. You have to understand that humans, based on deep psychological principles, value that for which they work for.

I’ll say it again. People value things they have to work for. How often has a guy come up to you in a bar, asking to buy you a drink, seemingly completely out of the blue? And how many times have you said yes, knowing full well, that once you’ve had the drink, you’ll give the guy no more attention? Why do you think that you’ve felt absolutely no attraction to the guy, even though he seemingly offered something of value, money, the drink to you? Because you didn’t have to work for it! It’s been proven time and time again in psychological studies, that people place a significantly higher value on things that they have had to work for!

Another example. Imagine you are applying to a new, prestigious job position. Your credentials seem to line up perfectly with what the employer is looking for, and your initial interview goes extremely well. The interviewer tells you that you’ll receive a call in the next few days to get started with the position. You get excited, telling all your friends that you’ve found your newest, high paying job. Except that you don’t hear back the next day. Or the next. Or the next. Eventually, a whole week goes by and you haven’t heard anything.

Frustrated, for which you thought it was a perfect match, you call the employer and ask what’s going on. They string you along, saying that some procedures took longer than expected, and you’ll have to come back in for another round of interviews.  Reluctantly you agree, wondering why they’re making you jump through hoops if they already said that you were exactly who they were looking for! Now, after going through that grueling, confusing process, how much harder do you think you’re going to work to make sure you keep the position? My guess is a hell of a lot harder! Because the employer made you seem dispensable at first, and then made you work extremely hard to get the position, you subconsciously began to question your abilities, and value the position even more!

The exact same line of reasoning applies to relationships. If you let your boyfriend have you too easily, and without much effort, he’ll think that you have low self-esteem and in turn value you less.

He arrives home, late, 5th time in a row, and finds Sally clearing off the table. He’ll say something empathetic like, I know you’re really frustrated and angry at me coming home late so often.

Sally will look at Jack and say something along the lines of agreement, I really am frustrated, and a little angry too. Jack will nod his head, accepting what she is saying, I know, and you have every right to be, but people are getting laid off at work and coming come early or on time is just really difficult right now because I’m so worried I’ll be next.

Suddenly, the little bit of anger that was in Sally would have melted away by now and she feels it’s her turn to comfort, I know there’s so much going on at work. Jack will cut her off here, because making excuses for his behavior is not part of the plan, Yeah well that’s no excuse. I don’t want to keep disappointing you. Tell me what I can do to make it up to you.

So Sally and Jack sit down at the dining room table and start talking, actually have a discussion where she can comfort him and talk about his stresses and he can comfort her and talk about her stresses, rather than end up having a bitter argument out of miscommunication.

The most important thing is that Jack never admits he was wrong in being late. He does stress how upset he is for disappointing Sally, and he does take responsibility for the upset in the relationship—but he doesn’t say he’s wrong.

This is the first step of learning how to use the Power of Apology to your benefit, learning how to take responsibility for your actions rather than use the defensive, make excuses justifying myself technique.

We’re going to go deeper into what to say a little later on, but before we get into that I want to tap into bad habits people have when it comes to making an apology. These traits are must avoids, meaning you should avoid them like the plague.

Pointing fingers. Stressing who’s in the wrong and who’s in the right is going to make things worse. Remember what we said about apology—you have to take responsibility of your actions.

Being insincere. Saying sorry just for the sake of saying sorry because you feel it’s what your partner expects is only going to backfire right at you. Same deal with going, I’m sorry, but. We learned this above:never make excuses. Take responsibility and give reasons—but never try to justify your actions because it only aggravates your partner more.

Demanding forgiveness. Some people expect instant forgiveness after making an apology, this is definitely not the case but we’ll go more into that a little later.

Demanding your partner admit he or she was in the wrong. True enough, using the above method , by taking responsibility of your apology, your partner may admit they were also contributing to the rift in your relationship, but you must not expect them to admit that they were in the wrong, or even expect them to start apologizing to you.

You need to take responsibility, if your partner had an affair you still need to take responsibility. Your partner may not be taking responsibility, but you can’t expect or demand them to take responsibility, unless you no longer want to be part of the relationship, of course, but this is all about salvaging your relationship so we won’t get into that.

Apologizing too soon. When someone should try to make an apology depends on how big the mistake was. If you went out and had one too many beers with your guy pals, a quick apology may be reasonable however, if you went out and had an affair, an instant apology will definitely seem insincere.

The most important factor in apologizing effectively is having the right mindset. With the right mindset, you’ll find that an effective apology will come naturally to you. 

Acknowledgement. When starting out your apology, it’s important to mention that you understand how the other person feels. A great way to do this is reverse your roles, and let’s go back to the Jack and Sally scenario. Imagine Jack spent al afternoon making dinner for Sally who came home late, not once or twice, but FIVE times in a row.

I think it’s safe to say Jack would be annoyed and angry. Now, in acknowledging their feelings you’re letting them know that you know how they feel. However a mere “I know how you feel…”isn’t going to cut it. In fact, it’s going to add more salt to the wound. To properly acknowledge their feelings, you should say something along the lines of, I know you feel, angry, lonely, hurt, etc.

How can someone be angry at you for understanding how they feel? In fact, after pulling them in this way, they may find the tables turned on them and in the end, it’s your partner who is comforting YOU.

How many times have you looked at your partner and said, “You look angry…”and your partner says something along the lines of, “I’m BEYOND angry, I’m confused, I’m upset and I’m hurt.”Or, sometimes, it goes something along the lines of, “I wouldn’t say I’m angry…just a little frustrated with you.”

Either way, you’ve won a deeper knowledge of how your partner is feeling.

The reason WHY. The next most important factor of your apology is your reason. You were late;all your partner can think about is WHY you were late. You had an affair;all your partner can think about is WHY you had the affair.

The why is critical and can be a very dangerous tool if you’re still using the old apology where you go on the defensive and start making excuses to justify your actions. Remember what we talked about up there:taking responsibility. You simply HAVE to take responsibility. How do you do this? You look at the problem and you spend time assessing what caused the problem to happen in the first place.

Let’s look at another scenario involving Jack and Sally. Sally has gone out and had an affair, and Jack wants to salvage what they have left of their relationship. No, I didn’t mistype that—indeed it was Sally who had the affair, but it’s Jack who’s going to take responsibility for this mistake.

So going back to what Jack has to do:he has to look at the problem and spend time assessing what caused the problem to happen in the first place—what did he do to contribute to it?

Jack may say something along the lines of, “You were feeling lonely and ignored…”.This is step one covered, you’ve acknowledged how they feel.

If Sally didn’t cut Jack off already, he will go along to say something along the lines of “I was swamped at work, I felt threatened because my boss had laid so many people off and I didn’t want to be the next one so instead I overworked myself and kept putting work before you and our family… I wasn’t around when you needed me…”. Step 2 is covered, you have taken responsibility of the problem.

At this point, both of them may burst into tears, Sally for finally being given the attention she needed and Jack for feeling the start of a better relationship with Sally on the horizon. Regardless of what happens in step 2, even if Sally threw herself in Jacks arms and everything seemed going as planned, you still have to move onto step 3.

Step 3 is acknowledging that you were affected by the mistake too—not by what they did, but for what you’re taking responsibility over. For instance, Jack could say something along the lines of, “I feel really disappointed in myself, hurt and guilty too that it was me who was responsible for your loneliness. But I feel lonely too, because my miscommunication and lack of dedication to our relationship cost me my lover, my best friend, my wife…”

The last step is apologizing. You cannot demand or expect forgiveness, but an apology still has to be made. Your partner may choose to never forgive you, but that’s a decision that is totally in their hands.

The apology is the most powerful step—it’s make it or break it here;either the power of apology works for you or doesn’t, but never in your apology appear as though you are EXPECTING or DEMANDING forgiveness.

Jack’s apology may go something along the lines of, “I realize that I can’t turn back the hands of time and do things differently, give you more attention, time, love and respect as much as I want to. I know I can’t erase the hurt and loneliness I caused you. And I know that I can’t expect forgiveness from you but I still hope you can find it in you to forgive me and give me another chance because I really am sorry…”
Let’s do a quick recap on the steps of this tactic:

  1. Acknowledgement of how they feel.
  2. The reason—what did you do that contributed to the mistake/problem
  3. This experience has affected you too. [IMPORTANT:what you did to contribute to it;never blame it on what THEY did!]
  4. A heartfelt apology that neither expects nor demands forgiveness. It also does not go on the defensive or make excuses up to justify the situation.

This method of apology isn’t just limited to your intimate relationship but can be used in ALL your relationships. For instance, let’s say you and your friend have conflicting political views and are having a really heated argument. You can stop, acknowledge how they feel, take responsibility of the argument, make a reason and apologize.

And remember, you don’t have to use this method in the big issues only either. If you have small outings on a regular basis, you can also use the Power of Apology to make sure they are not being piled up against each other and possibly used against you later on.

Looking Critically at Your Relationship

Congratulations, you’re almost done the book! Before you start the quest to get your ex back, and once you’ve finished this book, I want you to take a piece of paper and answer the following questions as completely as possible:

  • What is important to you in a boyfriend.
  • Why are those things important to you.
  • What do you find exciting about entering a new relationship.
  • What type of man do you want to enter a relationship with.
  • What do you fear about entering a relationship.
  • What do you fear about a man.
  • If somebody were to describe you as their ideal partner, how would they do so.
  • What do you enjoy most in life, and with whom.
  • What do you appreciate most about yourself.

It can take up to a day to finish all of these questions. But they are very deep, stirring queries that help you make sense of your soul. And, they will help you in looking critically at your relationship. Which of the things your wrote about did you have in your relationship? What things were you missing? Were you valued by your boy? Etc.

You have to judge yourself and the fit of your boyfriend before even trying to get him back. If, after answering these questions, you realize that your ex isn’t quite the man you thought he was, it’s time to let him go. You have to trust your judgment enough to select a great long-term partner for yourself.

For a powerful relationship, there definitely has to be a strong friendship behind the sexuality. That’s how you stay compatible with a guy for a long time, and – even though this is probably the most traditional advice you’ll find in this book – it’s great advice.

Passion

What are you passionate about in life? Does your boyfriend help you reach that passion? The two of you need to share a common vision for the future. This is so obvious that it is often overlooked. It is a key reason why relationships fail, and fail badly. Don’t jump into a relationship with the first hot guy you met. Don’t fall into that trap.

Be selective over who you chose to make your boyfriend, and make yourself a challenge. Figure out what you love to do in life, and with whom you love to do it with. Enjoy every moment, because you never know when it’ll all fade away. Realize that you’re not young forever, and take care of yourself to enjoy your youth.

You want to always look back at like and say “oh yea, that was a great time.” Find a man who shares all those qualities and get in a relationship with him. Even if there’s only one guy you know like that, maybe that’s the guy you really need. Poor couples who have bad relationships share no passions. Great couples with strong relationships share all their passions.

Fit into your boyfriend’s reality, and make him fit into yours. Those are the keys to a strong relationship.
